# BDEFS (Barkley Deficits in Executive Functioning Scale)

Created by Russell A. Barkley. A self-report assessment of executive function deficits in **daily life** — not a lab performance test. That's its key differentiator: it measures how EF failures actually show up in the real world.

## Five Domains

| Domain                            | What It Covers                                          |
| --------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Self-Management to Time           | Starting tasks, estimating duration, managing deadlines |
| Self-Organization/Problem Solving | Organizing materials, sequential planning               |
| Self-Restraint                    | Impulse control, inhibiting responses                   |
| Self-Motivation                   | Sustaining drive without external reward                |
| Self-Regulation of Emotion        | Not melting down over small things; emotional recovery  |

## Formats
- Adult (BDEFS) and child/adolescent (BDEFS-CA)
- Long-form (~15-20 min) and short-form (~4-5 min)
- Self-report and other-report versions
- Includes ADHD risk index for adults

## Why It Matters
More predictive of real-world functional impairment than traditional cognitive EF tests. Used to identify at-risk individuals, screen for ADHD, guide targeted interventions, and track outcomes.

## Reliability
Internal consistency (how consistently the scale measures what it claims to measure) is strong across all five domains, with omega coefficients ranging from .82 to .91. The five-domain structure has been validated in independent samples across different countries and age groups.

**Limitation:** It's self-report — people's ratings of themselves are imperfect. Scores on the BDEFS don't strongly predict scores on lab-based EF tests, and that's expected: the two things are measuring different aspects of the same underlying problem.

## Relationship to Other Models

The BDEFS is Barkley's ecological refinement of his own 1997 theoretical model (*ADHD and the Nature of Self-Control*). The 1997 model used different domain names and was organised around developmental sequence; the BDEFS reorganised the same theory around daily-life functional impairment. Key change: Barkley split his 1997 domain "self-regulation of affect/motivation/arousal" into two BDEFS domains — Self-Motivation and Self-Regulation of Emotion.

The BDEFS five domains map onto Miyake's three cold EF factors and extend beyond them:
- Self-Restraint → Inhibition
- Self-Org/Problem Solving + Time Management → Working Memory + Shifting
- Self-Motivation + Emotion Regulation → hot EF; no equivalent in Miyake's model

Dawson/Guare's 12 skills are a practitioner elaboration of Barkley's 1997 theory — the same theoretical root, translated into observable coaching targets.
