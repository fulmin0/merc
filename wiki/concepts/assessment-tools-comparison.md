# Assessment Tools Comparison: BDEFS, CAARS, Brown, Conners

Four widely-used adult ADHD and EF rating scales. Each operationalises different constructs, and they are not interchangeable.

---

## The Four Tools at a Glance

| | BDEFS | CAARS | Brown ADHD Scales | Conners' Adult (CAARS-L) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Full name** | Barkley Deficits in Executive Functioning Scale | Conners' Adult ADHD Rating Scales | Brown ADD Rating Scales for Adults | Conners' Adult ADHD Rating Scale — Long |
| **Author** | Russell Barkley | C. Keith Conners | Thomas Brown | C. Keith Conners |
| **Published** | 2011 | 1998 (rev. 2014) | 1996 | 1998 |
| **Items** | 89 (long), 20 (short) | 66 (long), 30 (short) | 40 | 66 |

| **DSM-aligned** | No | Yes | No | Yes |

---

## BDEFS

### What it measures
**Functional impairment** across Barkley's five daily-life EF domains: Self-Management to Time, Self-Organization/Problem Solving, Self-Restraint, Self-Motivation, Self-Regulation of Emotion. Measures how EF deficits actually affect real-world functioning rather than whether DSM criteria are met.

### Strengths
- Ecologically valid — captures real-world consequences, not abstract cognitive tasks
- Five-domain structure maps directly to intervention planning (which domain to target first)
- Self + other-report forms — discrepancies between rater perspectives are clinically informative
- Long + short form flexibility
- Normative data for ages 18–81

### Limitations
- Not DSM-aligned — does not map neatly to inattentive/hyperactive-impulsive presentation subtypes
- Less useful for insurance/diagnostic documentation than symptom-based scales
- Limited validation data outside North American populations

### Best used for
Profiling which EF domains are most impaired → prioritising interventions. Ongoing monitoring of functional change. Not a diagnostic tool in isolation.

---

## CAARS (Conners' Adult ADHD Rating Scales)

### What it measures
ADHD symptoms across six subscales: Inattention/Memory Problems, Hyperactivity/Restlessness, Impulsivity/Emotional Lability, Problems with Self-Concept, DSM-IV Inattentive Symptoms, DSM-IV Hyperactive-Impulsive Symptoms.

Includes an ADHD Index (18 items distinguishing ADHD from non-ADHD adults) and Inconsistency Index for response validity.

### Strengths
- DSM-aligned — facilitates diagnostic documentation and insurance coding
- Widely used; extensive normative database
- Self + observer versions; discrepancy analysis built in
- Inconsistency Index detects inconsistent responding
- Validated diagnostic accuracy: sensitivity ~70–80%, specificity ~70–80% against clinical ADHD diagnosis

### Limitations
- Poor sensitivity to non-credible responding (malingering or symptom exaggeration); Symptom Validity Testing studies show CAARS sensitivity to simulators is only 24–65%
- High false-positive rate in adults with anxiety or depression — internalising symptoms inflate scores into clinical range
- Subscale scores show variable factor structure across populations
- Cluster scores show "poor balance of sensitivity and specificity" in predicting ADHD when competing diagnoses are present

### Best used for
Initial screening; diagnostic assessment as part of comprehensive evaluation; monitoring symptom response to medication. Not a standalone diagnostic tool. Be cautious with results in high-anxiety or depressed populations.

## Brown ADD Rating Scales (BADDS)

### What it measures
Six cognitive/EF clusters: Activation (organising and starting work), Focus (sustaining and shifting attention), Effort (regulating alertness and effort), Emotion (managing frustration and modulating emotion), Memory (working memory and recall), Action (monitoring and regulating action).

Brown's model emphasises ADD as a disorder of cognitive management — closer to Barkley's functional model than to DSM symptom counts.

### Strengths
- Clinically nuanced — the six clusters map onto clinical presentations in a useful way
- Widely used in ADHD specialist practice; Brown's model is influential
- Covers emotional regulation explicitly (unlike original DSM criteria)
- Good face validity for patients — items resonate with self-reported experience

### Limitations
- Self-report only (no observer form in original)
- Less psychometric validation data than CAARS
- No short form
- Does not include symptom validity indicators

### Best used for
Clinical interviews and structured self-reflection. The six-cluster framework is particularly useful for explaining ADHD to patients in terms of daily functional failures rather than behavioural symptoms. Less suited to formal diagnostic assessment than CAARS.

## Key Comparison: BDEFS vs. CAARS vs. Brown

| Question | Best tool |
|---|---|
| Which EF domain is most impaired and should be targeted first? | BDEFS |
| Does this person meet DSM criteria for ADHD? | CAARS |
| What does daily functional failure actually look like for this person? | Brown or BDEFS |
| Is the other-rater's view consistent with self-report? | CAARS or BDEFS (both have observer forms) |
| Monitoring change in functional impairment over time | BDEFS short form |
| Monitoring change in symptom severity during medication trial | CAARS short form |

---

## What None of Them Do Well

**Malingering/non-credible responding.** CAARS sensitivity to simulators ranges from 24–65%. BDEFS and Brown have no formal validity indicators. For forensic or compensation contexts, the SRSI (Self-Report Symptom Inventory) or standalone Symptom Validity Tests should be added.

**Differential diagnosis.** All scales show significant score elevation in anxiety and depression. High scores alone cannot distinguish ADHD from mood/anxiety disorders. Structural clinical interview (DIVA, CAADID) remains necessary for diagnosis.

**Real-time performance.** These are all behavioural ratings — they measure perception of functioning, not cognitive performance. Neuropsychological testing (CPT, Stroop, WCST) captures different data and adds different information. The two approaches are complementary.

## For This Wiki's Purposes

The BDEFS is the primary assessment instrument for this knowledge base because:
1. It maps directly to Barkley's five-domain model used throughout the wiki
2. Its output drives intervention selection (domain × intervention matrix in [[concepts/bdefs-domains]])
3. It captures real-world functional impact, not abstract test performance

The CAARS is relevant if a formal diagnosis is needed for clinical, occupational, or insurance purposes.
