# Executive Function (EF)

Umbrella term for the set of cognitive processes that control goal-directed behavior. Not one thing — a family of related but separable capacities.

## Core Models

These frameworks are not competing alternatives — they operate at different levels of the same territory. → See [[concepts/framework-map]] for the full relationship.

### Miyake's Three-Factor Model (cognitive science layer)
The empirical workhorse. Three functions that are distinct yet correlated:

| Function | What It Does |
|----------|--------------|
| **Inhibition** | Stop yourself — resist automatic reactions, block distractions |
| **Updating (Working Memory)** | Hold and manipulate information in mind while using it |
| **Shifting (Cognitive Flexibility)** | Switch between tasks or approaches without getting stuck |

Validated in neurotypical adults using laboratory tasks. The academic standard for the structure of EF. Does not capture motivation or emotion — by design, not oversight.

### Barkley's Neuropsychological Theory (clinical layer)
Barkley (1997) argued cold EF is insufficient for ADHD. Behavioural inhibition is the prerequisite for all other EF capacities; its failure cascades into five developmental domains — including self-regulation of motivation and emotion, which Miyake's model does not cover. The BDEFS (2011) is his ecological assessment instrument built on this theory. → See [[concepts/bdefs]] and [[concepts/framework-map]]

### Dawson/Guare's 12-Skill Framework (practitioner layer)
Built explicitly on Barkley (1997). A practitioner translation of his theoretical domains into observable, coachable behaviours for school and coaching contexts. Not a separate theory — a different vocabulary for the same territory. → See [[strategies/dawson-guare-coaching]] and [[concepts/framework-map]]

### Other Frameworks
- **Lezak:** Volition, planning, purposive action, effective performance
- **Mateer (6 domains):** Initiation/drive, response inhibition, task persistence, organization, generative thinking, self-awareness
- **Norman & Shallice:** Supervisory Attentional System — centralized executive controller (earlier model, less dominant now)
- **Miller & Cohen:** PFC as goal-maintenance substrate

## Neural Architecture

**Key principle:** EF isn't in one place. The prefrontal cortex (PFC — the front part of the brain, behind the forehead) is central, but it works in a network with deeper brain structures.

| Region | Function |
|--------|----------|
| Dorsolateral PFC (outer/upper front) | Reasoning, planning, working memory |
| Ventromedial PFC (inner/lower front) | Emotion regulation, value-based decisions |

**Contemporary view:** EF has both unity (a shared underlying capacity) and diversity (separable parts). There is no single "executive" — it's a distributed network.

## EF Deficits in Daily Life

~89% of children with ADHD show impairment in at least one EF area; 75–85% show working memory problems specifically. Adults with ADHD consistently score below peers on EF measures — effect sizes in the range of d=0.62–0.89 across inhibition, verbal fluency, and set-shifting (Hervey et al., 2004, meta-analytic review — PubMed 16116936). Importantly, a significant proportion of adults with ADHD do not show measurable deficits on traditional EF tests; group averages mask substantial individual variation.

Real-world hits:
- Procrastination, missed deadlines (time management)
- Lost items, missed appointments (organization)
- Mental paralysis, inconsistent follow-through (task completion)
- Disproportionate emotional reactions, slow recovery (emotional regulation)
- Job instability, financial mismanagement (sustained performance)

Not laziness. Neural architecture.
