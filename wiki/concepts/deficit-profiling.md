# Deficit Profiling: How to Read the Map

No intervention works equally across all five BDEFS domains. The purpose of a deficit profile is to identify which domains are most impacted for a specific person — and then direct effort toward the interventions with the strongest match to those domains.

This page explains how profiling works and provides the domain-to-intervention map. It is a synthesis of the rest of the wiki — no new sources.

---

## Why It Matters

Without a profile, the intervention stack is generic. With one, it becomes directed: someone whose primary deficit is emotional regulation needs a different emphasis than someone whose primary deficit is time management. The underlying toolkit overlaps, but the starting points and priorities differ.

Profiling also helps calibrate severity. A person with mild time management difficulties can start with simple scaffolding. A person with severe deficits across multiple domains may need medication and structured therapy before the other layers are accessible.

---

## How to Build a Profile

### Option 1: The BDEFS (Most Direct)

The Barkley Deficits in Executive Functioning Scale produces a domain-by-domain score across all five areas. The short form takes about 5 minutes; the long form about 20.

- Available commercially from [Guilford Press](https://www.guilford.com) as part of the BDEFS-for-Adults manual
- Produces scores for each domain that can be compared to population norms
- Identifies which domains fall furthest below average

### Option 2: The Dawson/Guare Executive Skills Questionnaire

A self-rating questionnaire across 12 skills (see [[dawson-guare-coaching]]). Free versions exist online. Less formal than the BDEFS but more accessible and sufficient for a working profile.

### Option 3: Functional Analysis

Without a formal tool, a profile can be constructed from consequences:
- Where do failures cluster? (missed deadlines → time; lost items → organisation; emotional outbursts → regulation)
- Which problems are causing the most real-world damage? (work, relationships, finances, health)
- Which BDEFS domain descriptions below ring consistently true — not occasionally, but as a pattern?

## The Five Domains: Signatures and Indicators

### 1. Self-Management to Time
*The most commonly impacted domain.*

Indicators: chronic lateness, underestimating how long things take, starting too late, losing track of time entirely, deadlines that feel abstract until the last moment.

Distinguishing feature: the person often knows what time it is but cannot *feel* it passing. Time awareness is intellectual, not visceral.

---

### 2. Self-Organisation / Problem Solving
*Planning and managing the physical and mental environment.*

Indicators: persistent physical disorganisation, plans that collapse mid-execution, difficulty knowing what to do when an obstacle appears, struggling to work backwards from a goal to its steps.

Distinguishing feature: the person may be fully capable of complex thinking but cannot apply it sequentially without external structure.

---

### 3. Self-Restraint
*Inhibition — stopping, waiting, filtering.*

Indicators: speaking before thinking, impulsive decisions, difficulty stopping a behaviour once started, easily derailed by irrelevant stimuli, responding to the first thought rather than the best one.

Distinguishing feature: the person usually knows they should stop or wait — the problem is that the knowledge does not translate into the pause.

---

### 4. Self-Motivation
*Sustaining effort on tasks that are not immediately rewarding.*

Indicators: intense engagement with interesting tasks; near-total inability to engage with uninteresting ones regardless of importance. The person is not lazy — output in areas of genuine interest is often exceptional.

Distinguishing feature: motivation is not volitional. It is neurological. "Just try harder" is not a strategy here.

---

### 5. Self-Regulation of Emotion
*Managing the intensity and recovery time of emotional responses.*

Indicators: emotional reactions that feel disproportionate or arrive without warning, slow return to baseline after being upset, sensitivity to criticism or rejection, avoidance of situations that might produce negative emotion.

Distinguishing feature: the problem is not having emotions — it is that the brain's mechanism for modulating their intensity is underperforming.

## How Domains Interact

Deficits rarely appear in isolation. Common combinations:

**Time + Motivation:** The two most commonly co-occurring domains in adults. Time blindness makes deadlines non-urgent; low motivation makes starting difficult. Together they produce consistent underperformance on low-interest tasks.

**Restraint + Emotion:** Share neural circuitry. Difficulty inhibiting impulses and difficulty managing emotional intensity are mechanistically related. Gains in one often improve the other.

**Organisation + Time:** Planning without time awareness collapses. Systems designed to compensate for both simultaneously are more robust than systems targeting either alone (see Solanto Module 1–2).

**Emotion as amplifier:** When emotional regulation is significantly impaired, it degrades function across all other domains. Emotional flooding consumes working memory and disrupts even well-established scaffolds. Addressing emotion regulation early — especially if it is causing the most visible harm — often unlocks progress in the other domains.

## Using the Profile Across Different Severity Levels

The profile is not static. EF functioning varies with:
- Sleep quality
- Stress load
- Life transitions (new job, relationship changes, illness)
- Medication status

A practical approach is to think in terms of *current capacity*, not fixed deficit. The interventions that are accessible during a high-functioning period (MCT, exercise habit) are different from those that are accessible during a low-functioning period (simple scaffolding, medication if available).

Designing systems that work at low capacity is more important than designing systems that work only at high capacity.
