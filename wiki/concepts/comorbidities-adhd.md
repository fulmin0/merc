# Comorbidities: ADHD and Co-occurring Conditions

## Why It Matters

ADHD rarely presents alone. ~70% of adults with ADHD carry at least one additional psychiatric diagnosis. Comorbidities compound EF deficits, complicate diagnosis, and alter treatment sequencing. Treating ADHD without addressing active comorbidities typically produces partial or unstable results.

---

## Prevalence Overview

From Choi et al. (2022, *PLoS One*, systematic review, n=40,103+ across included studies):

| Condition | Prevalence in ADHD adults | Prevalence without ADHD |
|---|---|---|
| Any anxiety disorder | 4.3–47% (gen pop), 3.9–84% (clinical) | 0.5–9.5% (gen pop) |
| Depressive disorder | 8.6–55% (gen pop), 15–40% (clinical) | 1.2–12.5% (gen pop) |
| Bipolar disorder | 4.5–35% (gen pop), 7.4–80% (clinical) | 0.2–3.6% (gen pop); OR up to 29.94 |
| Substance use disorder | 2.3–41% (gen pop) | 0–16.6% (gen pop) |
| Personality disorder | 0.3–34% (gen pop), 22–66% (clinical) | 0–3.9% (gen pop) |

Anxiety and depression are the most common and most practically relevant for EF management.

---

## Anxiety Disorders

### Prevalence
47–56% of adults with ADHD have at least one anxiety disorder (Fu et al., 2025, *Frontiers in Psychiatry*, PMID 40547117). Rates are higher in females.

### Interaction with EF
Anxiety and ADHD share "cold" cognitive deficits: selective attention, working memory, verbal fluency. The combination produces additive impairment — neither condition fully explains the other's EF profile.

Specific interaction effects:
- Anxiety worsens sleep, which further degrades EF (see [[concepts/sleep-and-ef]])
- Cognitive rigidity and rumination link EF deficits to depression risk via anxiety as an intermediate
- Delayed PFC maturation in ADHD creates particular adolescent-to-adult vulnerability

### Diagnostic note
Anxiety in ADHD is often secondary — driven by accumulated failure, avoidance cycles, and the chronic stress of mismanaged demands. This distinction matters for treatment: addressing root EF failures may reduce anxiety without anxiety-specific treatment. True comorbid anxiety disorder requires its own intervention.

### Treatment
- Treat the more impairing condition first (clinical guideline consensus)
- CBT effective for both; can be sequenced or integrated
- Stimulants generally safe with anxiety comorbidity; non-stimulants (atomoxetine) may be preferred where anxiety is primary

## Depressive Disorders

### Prevalence
18.6–53.3% of adults with ADHD have comorbid depression. Higher rates in females.

### Interaction with EF
Depression and ADHD both increase delay discounting (preference for immediate over delayed rewards), but via different mechanisms:
- ADHD: reward system hypersensitivity to immediate rewards, dopamine-mediated
- Depression: pessimistic future orientation reduces the subjective value of future rewards

The overlap produces compounded motivation failure — initiation is impaired from both ends.

Cognitive rigidity and rumination — features of depression — directly worsen metacognition and flexible problem-solving, two of the most clinically relevant EF domains.

### Diagnostic note
Distinguishing primary ADHD with secondary depression from primary depression with attention symptoms requires longitudinal history. ADHD onset is typically childhood; depressive episodes often began post-adolescence after accumulated executive failure. When in doubt, treat the more severe and unstable condition first.

### Treatment
- Sequential or combined pharmacotherapy (stimulants + antidepressants)
- CBT adapted for ADHD (structured session format, externalised reminders)
- Antidepressants alone without ADHD treatment typically produce incomplete response

## Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

### Prevalence
50–70% of adults with ASD have co-occurring ADHD. 15–50% of adults with ADHD show autistic traits or meet ASD diagnostic criteria (Townes et al., 2023, systematic review + meta-analysis).

This is not diagnostic overlap — these are genuinely co-occurring distinct conditions that now can be coded simultaneously (DSM-5, 2013).

### EF Profile: Shared and Distinct

Both conditions impair EF, but with somewhat different signatures:

| Domain | ADHD | ASD | ASD + ADHD |
|---|---|---|---|
| Response inhibition | Impaired (impulsive style) | Impaired (slow/accurate style) | Additive impairment |
| Cognitive flexibility | Moderate impairment | More severe impairment | Shares ASD severity |
| Planning | Impaired | Impaired | No difference between groups |
| Working memory | Impaired | Impaired | Additive impairment |
| Attention | Impaired | Impaired | No clear distinction |

Key finding: no significant difference in EF profiles between ASD and ADHD when measured neuropsychologically. Differences emerge on behavioural questionnaires: ADHD-rated higher on behavioural dysregulation; ASD higher on rigidity and social processing demands.

### Practical implications
- ASD accommodation (predictable structure, clear expectations, reduced social ambiguity) overlaps substantially with ADHD scaffolding — the interventions are largely compatible
- Social skills peer coaching (see [[strategies/notes-coaching-students-ef-deficits]]) may require more explicit instruction for autistic individuals; the ADHD assumption of "knows but doesn't do" does not reliably hold
- Sensory and processing demands compound executive load in ASD + ADHD; environment design matters more

## Bipolar Disorder Type II

### Prevalence
~8% of adults with ADHD have a comorbid bipolar disorder diagnosis (7.9% in Schiweck et al. systematic review of 650,000 participants). Conversely, 17.1% of those with bipolar disorder have ADHD. Odds ratio for bipolar in ADHD adults is up to 29.94 vs. general population (Choi et al., 2022).

Rates of Bipolar II specifically are higher than Bipolar I in the ADHD population. The hyperactivity and emotional dysregulation features of ADHD overlap most closely with the hypomanic episodes of Bipolar II.

### Differential Diagnosis

Both ADHD and Bipolar II produce: distractibility, impulsivity, rapid speech, and emotional dysregulation. Correct distinction matters critically for treatment.

Key distinguishing features:

| Feature | ADHD | Bipolar II |
|---|---|---|
| Onset | Childhood (before age 12) | Typically adult onset (though may present in adolescence) |
| Course | Chronic, stable baseline | Episodic — distinct periods of elevation and depression |
| Mood elevation | Reactive (situational), not sustained | Distinct hypomanic episodes lasting days–weeks |
| Sleep | Disrupted (DSPS, insomnia) | Markedly decreased need for sleep during hypomania |
| Impulsivity | Chronic and consistent | Episodic, more severe during hypomania |
| Response to stimulants | Typically improves function | May precipitate or worsen mood episodes without mood stabiliser |

Both may coexist — comorbidity is confirmed when ADHD symptoms are clearly present outside mood episodes.

### EF Profile
Both conditions impair EF, particularly on tasks with high working memory demand. Cognitive problems were once attributed solely to ADHD; they are now understood as intrinsic to bipolar disorder as well. Comorbid cases show additive impairment and worse overall functioning.

### Clinical outcome
Adults with ADHD + bipolar disorder show: earlier bipolar onset, more mood episodes, higher substance abuse rates, lower quality of life, and worse overall functioning than either condition alone (Parker, 2025, *Australasian Psychiatry*, PMID 40512207).

### Treatment
- **Bipolar stabilisation first.** Mood stability must be established before ADHD treatment begins.
- Stimulants are contraindicated without concurrent mood stabiliser; they may precipitate hypomanic/manic episodes.
- Once stabilised on a mood stabiliser, stimulant introduction is feasible with monitoring.
- Do not initiate ADHD-focused EF interventions (coaching, MCT) during active mood episodes — wait for stability.

---

## Treatment Sequencing Principle

The clinical consensus (Fu et al., 2025; Parker, 2025):

**Treat the most severe, functionally impairing, and unstable condition first.**

Practical hierarchy for EF-focused intervention:
1. Active mood episode (bipolar) → stabilise first; everything else waits
2. Severe depression → treat before or alongside ADHD
3. Active substance use → address before expecting ADHD treatment to hold
4. Anxiety, mild-moderate depression → can be treated concurrently with ADHD
