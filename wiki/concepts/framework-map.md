# EF Framework Map: Miyake, Barkley, and Dawson/Guare

How the three major EF frameworks relate. They are not competing alternatives — they operate at different levels of the same territory.

---

## The Four Levels

| Level | Framework | What it answers | Validated via |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cognitive science | Miyake et al. (2000) — 3 factors | What EF *is* at the computational level | Lab tasks (Stroop, n-back, task-switching) |
| Neuropsychological theory | Barkley (1997) — 5 developmental domains | How EF develops; why inhibition is the prerequisite | Clinical and developmental research; ADHD literature |
| Practitioner translation | Dawson/Guare (2004+) — 12 skills | Which observable skills can be coached | School/clinical practice; ESQ validation |
| Ecological assessment | Barkley (2011 BDEFS) — 5 daily-life domains | How EF deficits show up in real-world functioning | BDEFS psychometric validation |

Miyake sits underneath as the cognitive science foundation. Barkley (1997) is the clinical theory that explains ADHD specifically. D/G is a practitioner elaboration of Barkley (1997). The BDEFS is Barkley's own ecological assessment of that same theory, refined for daily-life measurement.

---

## Miyake: The Cognitive Science Layer

Miyake et al. (2000) studied EF in neurotypical adults using a battery of laboratory tasks. Factor analysis identified three separable but correlated functions:

- **Inhibition** — suppressing prepotent (automatic) responses
- **Updating** — holding and manipulating information in working memory
- **Shifting** — switching between tasks or mental sets without perseveration

This is the most empirically validated structure of EF at the cognitive level. It is the standard in academic and research contexts.

**What it doesn't capture:** motivation and emotion. Miyake's lab paradigm uses purely cognitive tasks — it was never designed to measure why someone can't start a task they find aversive, or why they emotionally dysregulate under pressure. These are "hot" EF capacities that require a different framework.

---

## Barkley 1997: The Neuropsychological Theory Layer

Barkley's 1997 model (*ADHD and the Nature of Self-Control*) is the theoretical architecture that explains ADHD as an EF disorder. His central argument: **behavioural inhibition is the prerequisite for all other EF capacities** — it must develop first, and its failure cascades into every other domain.

His five developmental domains:

| Domain | What it covers |
|---|---|
| Behavioural inhibition | Suppressing prepotent responses; the foundational prerequisite |
| Non-verbal working memory | Holding events in mind; time perception; foresight |
| Verbal working memory | Internalisation of speech; self-instruction; rule-following |
| Self-regulation of affect/motivation/arousal | Managing emotional and motivational states toward goals |
| Reconstitution | Analysis and synthesis of behaviour; fluency; flexible problem-solving |

**The key claim Barkley makes against Miyake:** cold EF (inhibition, updating, shifting) is insufficient to explain ADHD. Self-regulation of affect and motivation — domain 4 — is not a downstream effect; it is a core EF capacity. The most disabling features of ADHD (motivational inconsistency, emotional dysregulation) live here. A model that doesn't include them is incomplete for clinical purposes.

---

## Dawson/Guare: The Practitioner Translation Layer

Dawson and Guare were neuropsychologists working in schools and attention disorder clinics. They built their 12-skill framework explicitly on Barkley's 1997 model — their books open by walking through Barkley's developmental model as the theoretical foundation before introducing their own vocabulary.

Their goal was not theoretical contribution. They needed a vocabulary that teachers, coaches, and parents could use to identify, observe, and target specific behaviours. Twelve skills are more granular and more action-oriented than five theoretical domains.

**Confirmed by the ESQ-R validation study (PMC8430784):** "The ESQ-R measures executive functioning skills as opposed to core executive functions (such as working memory, inhibition, and shifting/flexibility; Miyake et al., 2000), with these core executive functions facilitating the higher-order executive functioning skills that the measure assesses."

In other words: Miyake's three factors are the underlying cognitive machinery; D/G's twelve skills are the surface behaviours that emerge from it.

**One empirical note:** when the ESQ is factor-analysed, it does not recover twelve independent factors. It recovers approximately five second-order factors — much closer to Barkley's structure than to the face-valid twelve-skill list. The twelve skills are useful observational categories, not twelve neurologically distinct constructs.

---

## Barkley 2011 BDEFS: The Ecological Assessment Layer

The BDEFS is Barkley's own refinement of his 1997 theory into a daily-life measurement instrument. It retains five domains but reorganises them around functional impairment rather than developmental sequence:

| BDEFS domain (2011) | Barkley 1997 origin |
|---|---|
| Self-Restraint | Behavioural inhibition |
| Self-Management to Time | Non-verbal working memory (time is held via non-verbal WM) |
| Self-Organization/Problem Solving | Reconstitution + verbal working memory |
| Self-Motivation | Self-regulation of affect/motivation (motivation component) |
| Self-Regulation of Emotion | Self-regulation of affect/motivation (affect component) |

Note: Barkley split domain 4 of his 1997 model (self-regulation of affect/motivation/arousal) into two BDEFS domains — Self-Motivation and Self-Regulation of Emotion. This is a practical refinement, not a theoretical change.

## Framework Mapping Table

| Miyake (2000) | Barkley 1997 | Dawson/Guare 12 skills | Barkley BDEFS (2011) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Inhibition | Behavioural inhibition | Response Inhibition | Self-Restraint |
| Updating (WM) | Non-verbal WM | Working Memory, Time Management | Self-Management to Time |
| Shifting | Reconstitution (partial) | Flexibility, Metacognition | Self-Organization/Problem Solving (partial) |
| Updating + Shifting | Verbal WM + Reconstitution | Planning/Prioritization, Organization, Task Initiation | Self-Organization/Problem Solving |
| *(no equivalent)* | Self-reg of affect/motivation | Emotional Control, Stress Tolerance | Self-Regulation of Emotion |
| *(no equivalent)* | Self-reg of affect/motivation | Goal-Directed Persistence, Sustained Attention | Self-Motivation |

**Where mappings are clean:** Miyake Inhibition ↔ Barkley behavioural inhibition ↔ D/G Response Inhibition ↔ BDEFS Self-Restraint. The closest 1:1 in the whole table.

**Where mappings are loose:** D/G's Organisation and Task Initiation draw from multiple Barkley 1997 domains. D/G's Metacognition has no single clean equivalent — it cuts across reconstitution and verbal working memory.

**Where Miyake has no coverage:** Domains 4 and 5 of the BDEFS (Self-Motivation, Emotion Regulation) and their D/G equivalents. These are the hot EF capacities. For ADHD specifically, they are often the most functionally impairing.

## Which Framework for Which Purpose

| Question | Framework to use |
|---|---|
| What is EF at a computational level? | Miyake (2000) |
| Why does inhibition failure cascade into everything else? | Barkley (1997) theoretical model |
| What specifically is this person struggling with day-to-day? | BDEFS (2011) — ecological assessment |
| What skills should a coach or teacher target? | Dawson/Guare ESQ |
| Which interventions target which deficits? | BDEFS domain × intervention matrix |
