# Sleep and Executive Function

## What It Is

Sleep deprivation and EF deficits are deeply entangled. The relationship runs in both directions: poor sleep impairs EF, and EF deficits (particularly in ADHD) disrupt sleep architecture and circadian timing. This makes sleep one of the highest-leverage intervention points in the EF stack.

---

## How Sleep Deprivation Impairs EF

Sleep deprivation preferentially damages prefrontal-cortex-dependent functions. The PFC is the most metabolically demanding and sleep-sensitive region of the brain.

**Effect sizes (Misra et al., 2025 — meta-analysis, 39 studies, n=4,578):**

| EF Domain | Hedges' g | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Overall EF | −0.62 | Moderate-to-large |
| Working memory | −0.71 | Large |
| Inhibitory control | −0.59 | Moderate |
| Cognitive flexibility | −0.49 | Moderate |

These are effect sizes comparable to the EF deficits measured in ADHD itself. A single night of significant deprivation is not a minor inconvenience.

**Dose-response (Sen & Tai, 2023 — review, ~474,417 UK Biobank participants):**

- Optimal duration: 7–8 hours — EF peaks here
- Inverted-U relationship: both short *and* long sleep degrade EF
- Every hour below or above 7h correlates with parametric EF decline
- Short sleep (odds ratio 1.40) and long sleep (OR 1.58) both increase odds of cognitive impairment

**Sleep quality matters more than duration.** Sleep efficiency and fragmentation predict EF performance more strongly than total sleep time. Six hours of unbroken sleep likely outperforms seven hours of fragmented sleep.

**Neural mechanisms:**

Three converging pathways:
1. **PFC metabolism:** sleep deprivation reduces cerebral blood flow to prefrontal regions; fMRI shows reduced PFC activation on cognitive flexibility tasks
2. **Dopamine:** sleep restriction lowers dopamine availability — directly overlapping with the neurotransmitter system implicated in ADHD
3. **Alertness baseline:** selective attention shows the largest effect size (ηp² = 0.41, García et al., 2021); sustained attention and cognitive inhibition follow

## The ADHD–Sleep Bidirectional Loop

ADHD does not merely coexist with sleep problems — each makes the other worse.

**Prevalence (van der Ham et al., 2024; Sleep Foundation review):**

- ~60% of adults with ADHD screen positive for at least one sleep disorder
- Specific disorders:
  - Delayed Sleep Phase Syndrome: 36%
  - Insomnia: 30–44%
  - Restless Legs Syndrome / PLMD: 29%
  - Sleep-disordered breathing (apnea): up to 33%

**Circadian dysregulation is the central mechanism.** Adults with ADHD show a dim-light melatonin onset (DLMO — the reliable biological marker of circadian phase) delayed by approximately 90 minutes compared to controls (Surman & Walsh, 2021). This means the internal clock is running late. The result: difficulty falling asleep at conventional times, difficulty waking, morning cognitive fog, and an evening "second wind" that further delays sleep.

**The bidirectional amplification:**
- ADHD → delayed DLMO, poor sleep initiation, fragmented sleep → worsened EF the following day → more difficulty managing the day → more evening activation → delayed sleep → worse ADHD symptoms
- Sleep deprivation alone can push subclinical ADHD inattention scores into clinical range (Cassoff et al., 2012)

**Dopamine as the common substrate.** Sleep deprivation reduces dopamine availability. ADHD involves dopamine system dysregulation. Insufficient sleep and ADHD may compound each other through the same neurochemical pathway — which explains why sleep problems worsen ADHD symptoms beyond what the circadian disruption alone would predict.

## Interventions

### Morning Bright Light Therapy (best evidence)

**Van Andel et al. (2022) — RCT, adults with ADHD + DSPD:**
- Melatonin + morning bright light → DLMO advanced by ~2 hours (p<0.001)
- ADHD symptoms reduced 14% (p=0.038)

**Fargason et al. (2017) — pilot RCT:**
- 2 weeks, 10,000 lux lamp in the morning
- DLMO advanced 31 minutes (p=0.002)
- Sleep quality improved (p<0.001)

Protocol: 20–30 min of 10,000 lux bright light exposure within 30 minutes of waking. Most effective at advancing circadian phase, particularly for delayed DLMO.

### CBT-I with ADHD Adaptations

CBT-I (cognitive-behavioural therapy for insomnia) is the evidence-based standard for chronic insomnia. For ADHD adults it requires accommodation: written reminders, alarms, and reduced cognitive load in session exercises. Sleep hygiene alone is insufficient for chronic insomnia — behaviour change without restructuring dysfunctional sleep cognitions does not hold.

**Jernelov et al. (2019):** CBT-I reduced insomnia severity significantly (p=0.002); gains maintained at 3-month follow-up.

### Fixed Wake Time

The single highest-leverage behavioural anchor. Setting and holding a consistent wake time (even on weekends) stabilises the circadian system more effectively than controlling bedtime. Bedtime varies; wake time should not.

### Evening Light Restriction

Blue light from screens suppresses melatonin. For adults with already-delayed DLMO, evening light exposure extends the delay further. Screen hygiene 1–2 hours before target bedtime, warm-toned lighting in the evening.

### Low-Dose Melatonin

For confirmed or suspected DLMO delay: low-dose melatonin (0.5–1mg, not the 5–10mg doses common in commercial products) taken 5–6 hours before target bedtime — not at bedtime. Taking melatonin at bedtime may sedate but does not advance the clock. Timing matters more than dose.

Not a substitute for circadian anchoring. Use alongside bright light and fixed wake time.

### What Not To Do First

Sedating medications address symptoms without addressing the underlying circadian displacement. They can create dependency and do not correct the DLMO delay. Appropriate as escalation when behavioural and circadian interventions have been tried.

## Practical Priority

For anyone with EF deficits — with or without ADHD — sleep is a foundational variable before any other intervention can operate at full capacity. Scaffolding on top of chronic sleep deprivation is like building on sand.

**Minimum viable changes, in order:**
1. Fixed wake time — every day, no exceptions
2. Morning light — 20–30 min within 30 min of waking
3. Evening screen curfew — 60–90 min before target bedtime
4. Evaluate duration — aim for 7–8 hours of *sleep* (not time in bed)
5. If insomnia persists after 2–4 weeks of the above: consider CBT-I

---

## Where It Fits in the Stack

Sleep is not an intervention in the same sense as scaffolding or exercise — it is a prerequisite. Deficits in sleep quality or circadian timing will suppress the return from every other layer.

Position: **below the stack**. Address before optimising the other layers.

| Layer | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Sleep** | Prerequisite — enables everything above it |
| Scaffolding | Routes around deficits |
| Exercise | Builds structural capacity |
| Medication | Removes chemical bottleneck |
| MCT/coaching | Teaches self-maintenance |
