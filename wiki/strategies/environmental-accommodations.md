# Environmental Accommodations

The environment is a scaffold. Changing what surrounds you changes how much EF you need to spend. No RCTs exist for specific environmental modifications in adults with ADHD — the evidence here is expert consensus and practitioner experience, consistent with the broader scaffolding literature.

The underlying principle: **reduce the number of decisions and demands the environment places on you, especially during low-capacity moments.**

---

## Noise and Distraction

Auditory distraction is one of the most consistently reported problems. The fix is not silence — many people with ADHD focus better with consistent background sound than in complete quiet.

| Situation | Approach |
|---|---|
| Open office / uncontrolled environment | Noise-cancelling headphones + consistent audio (white noise, music without lyrics, Brain.fm) |
| Home workspace | Designate a single space for focused work — one room or corner, used consistently |
| Unpredictable interruptions | Visible "do not disturb" signal; communicate focus blocks to housemates/colleagues |

**What doesn't help:** Trying to work through interruptions. Adults with ADHD show impaired attentional set-shifting — the cost of reorienting after a switch is greater than in neurotypical peers (PMC6230251). Prevention is more effective than recovery.

---

## Visual Environment

Visual clutter competes for attention. Bright, high-contrast environments amplify distraction. Clear surfaces reduce the number of things the brain registers as potential tasks or stimuli.

Practical changes:
- Keep the desk clear of anything not relevant to the current task
- Face a wall, not a window or doorway, if visual movement is distracting
- Use physical inboxes (one for incoming, one for filing) rather than piles — piles are invisible to ADHD brains after the first day

**Visible organising systems beat invisible ones.** If you can't see it, it doesn't exist. Open shelves, transparent containers, and labelled drawers reduce the working memory required to find things.

---

## Structural Predictability

Unpredictability is cognitively expensive. The more routine a day is, the less EF is spent on orientation.

- Fixed start times, meal times, and wind-down routines reduce daily decision fatigue
- Pre-planned transitions ("after lunch I go to the desk") replace open-ended choices
- Keeping frequently used items in a fixed location removes the search-and-find tax

This overlaps with Solanto's Session 9 (routines) — see [[solanto-mct]].

## Workplace Accommodations

For those in employment, formal accommodations under disability frameworks may be available. Common and effective requests:

| Accommodation | What it addresses |
|---|---|
| Quiet workspace or private office | Auditory and visual distraction |
| Flexible hours / async work | Peak performance windows vary; rigidity wastes capacity |
| Written briefs and agendas | Reduces working memory load in meetings |
| Deadline reminders from manager | External time structure |
| Ability to work remotely | Allows custom environment design |
| Shorter, more frequent check-ins | Replaces long planning cycles with small feedback loops |

Most of these cost nothing. The barrier is disclosure — which carries its own risks and requires individual judgement.

---

## The Order of Operations

Environmental changes are often the fastest and cheapest intervention available. Unlike building habits or starting a therapy program, most can be implemented today.

- Start with noise: headphones and a consistent audio environment
- Then visual simplification: clear the surface, remove non-task items
- Then structural predictability: anchor two or three daily routines

If the environment is fighting you, the other interventions (exercise, mindfulness, MCT) are harder to maintain. Fix the environment first.

---

## Evidence Note

No randomised controlled trials exist for specific environmental modifications in adults with ADHD. The recommendations here are derived from:
- The broader scaffolding literature (strong evidence — see [[scaffolding-vs-training]])
- Expert consensus from ADHD practitioners (Job Accommodation Network, ADDA)
- Qualitative research on adult ADHD coping strategies

Evidence tier 4 reflects the absence of adult RCTs, not the absence of practical value.
