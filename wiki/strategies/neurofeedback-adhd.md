# Neurofeedback for ADHD

## What It Is

Neurofeedback (EEG biofeedback) trains self-regulation of brainwave activity. Electrodes placed on the scalp measure EEG in real time; the patient receives feedback (audio, video game, visual cue) when their brain produces desired activity patterns. The aim is to shift habitual EEG patterns toward those associated with better attention and executive control.

Not medication, not therapy. Positioned by practitioners as a means of directly training the neural substrate of attention.

---

## The Theoretical Basis

ADHD is associated with elevated theta waves (4–8 Hz, associated with drowsiness and mind-wandering) and reduced beta waves (13–30 Hz, associated with alert, focused processing) in frontal regions — elevated theta/beta ratio.

**Standard protocols:**

- **Theta/beta (TBR):** suppress theta, enhance beta at the vertex (Cz electrode). Most commonly used. Addresses the theta-excess signature.
- **Slow Cortical Potential (SCP):** train cortical excitability — negative SCPs increase cortical activation; positive SCPs reduce it. More cognitively demanding to learn.
- **Alpha/theta:** increase theta relative to alpha — used for relaxation/emotional regulation rather than attention enhancement.
- **Combined dual-protocol:** SCP + TBR sequentially. Evidence suggests superior to either alone.

The mechanism theory is plausible. The question is whether training the EEG signal actually produces durable, generalised behavioural change — or whether the effect is specific to the training task, or driven by expectation.

## The Evidence

### Core findings

**Westwood et al. (2025) — JAMA Psychiatry — the authoritative current review:**

38 RCTs, n=2,472, ages 5–40. European ADHD Guidelines Group (EAGG).

| Outcome | Studies | N | SMD | 95% CI | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Total symptoms (probably blinded) | 20 | 1,214 | 0.04 | −0.10 to 0.18 | ns |
| Standard protocol subgroup | 9 | 681 | 0.21 | 0.02 to 0.40 | p<0.05 |
| Processing speed (EF) | 15 | 909 | 0.35 | 0.01 to 0.69 | p<0.05 |
| Other EF domains | — | — | — | — | ns |

**Conclusion from the authors:** *"Neurofeedback did not appear to meaningfully benefit individuals with ADHD, clinically or neuropsychologically, at the group level."*

The small effect in the standard-protocol subgroup (SMD=0.21) is of borderline clinical significance and derived from 9 studies, introducing selection concerns.

### The blinding problem

Chung et al. (2022, *Journal of Psychiatry & Neuroscience*, 21 RCTs, n=1,261) is typical of studies that show positive results: inattention, hyperactivity, and global symptoms all significantly improved vs. controls — but only in studies using wait-list or treatment-as-usual comparators. Subgroup analysis adopting a double-blind design **failed to demonstrate superiority over sham feedback**.

This is a consistent pattern across the neurofeedback literature. Effect sizes shrink or disappear when:
- Raters are blinded to treatment allocation
- Active sham control is used (rather than waitlist)
- Assessment is conducted by someone other than the treating clinician

The implication is that a substantial portion of observed benefit is expectation, therapeutic alliance, and attention from a practitioner — not the EEG training itself.

### Adults specifically

The adult-specific evidence is weaker still. A pilot meta-analysis of 5 RCTs (n=279, mean age 23.48) found **no difference** between neurofeedback and comparison conditions on inattention, hyperactivity/impulsivity, or total symptoms in adolescents and adults (Scientific Reports, 2022).

### Children vs. adults

The more optimistic findings come from child studies — particularly on inhibitory control and working memory — with effects appearing only after substantial training (>1,260 minutes, roughly 42+ sessions of 30 min). This dose-duration threshold does not translate straightforwardly to adults, and no equivalent adult meta-analysis confirms similar effects.

### Moderators

From Chung et al. (2022):
- Higher IQ associated with better treatment response
- Combined dual protocols (SCP + TBR) outperform single-protocol
- Wait-list controls inflate effects vs. sham controls

## Practical Assessment

**Cost:** Typically 40–60 sessions required for any claim of efficacy. Market rate is £80–200/session. Total cost: £3,000–12,000. No NHS provision for adults.

**Time:** 40 sessions × 45–60 min = 30–40 hours in-clinic, often over 3–6 months.

**Return:** On the best available blinded evidence: near-zero effect on ADHD symptoms. Possible small improvement in processing speed. No confirmed working memory or inhibitory control benefit in adults.

**Comparison:** The same investment of time and money applied to CBT-I, coached scaffolding, or supervised exercise would produce effects with substantially stronger evidential support.

---

## Verdict

**Not currently recommended for adults with EF deficits or ADHD.**

The theoretical basis is coherent. The effect disappears under rigorous blinding. The adult-specific evidence is null. The cost is high and the evidence insufficient to justify it over well-evidenced alternatives.

This position may warrant revision if adult-specific, properly blinded RCTs with adequate training dose emerge. The field is active; the 2025 JAMA Psychiatry meta-analysis is the current ceiling of evidence.

**Not placed in the stack.** If exploring experimental options after exhausting the main stack layers, discuss with a clinician rather than pursuing independently.

## Open Questions

1. Does prolonged training (>1,200 min) produce different outcomes in adults than shorter protocols?
2. Are there EEG responder subgroups — individuals whose theta/beta ratio is actually elevated — who benefit more reliably?
3. Do combined SCP+TBR protocols perform differently from TBR alone in adults specifically?
