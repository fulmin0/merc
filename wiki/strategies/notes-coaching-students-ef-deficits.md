# Notes: Coaching Students with Executive Skills Deficits

**Dawson, P. & Guare, R. (2012). *Coaching Students with Executive Skills Deficits.* Guilford Press. ISBN 978-1-4625-0375-9.**

Primary text read directly. These are working notes — more granular than [[strategies/dawson-guare-coaching|the summary page]].

---

## Part I: The Coaching Model

### Ch. 1 — What Is Coaching?

Coaching is not therapy, tutoring, or instruction. It is an external structure that helps students build and maintain goal-directed behavior.

Core framing — the sports-coach analogy (p.27, direct quote):

> "Academic coaches work sort of like sports coaches: They help you decide what you want to achieve and then meet with you on a regular basis to make sure you're on track to reach your goals."

The ultimate goal of coaching is **self-determination** — defined by Field et al. (1998) as "a combination of skills, knowledge and beliefs that enable a person to engage in goal-directed, self-regulated, autonomous behavior."

Coaching works toward its own obsolescence: a successful coaching relationship eventually fades itself out as the student internalises the structure.

---

### Ch. 2 — The 11-Skill Framework

The coaching book uses **11 executive skills** (the adult adaptation adds Stress Tolerance for 12):

1. Response Inhibition
2. Working Memory
3. Emotional Control
4. Sustained Attention
5. Task Initiation
6. Planning/Prioritization
7. Organization
8. Time Management
9. Flexibility
10. Goal-Directed Persistence
11. Metacognition

Note on sustained attention: Barkley (1997) distinguishes *context-dependent* sustained attention (novelty or interest-driven) from *goal-directed* sustained attention (what EF deficits actually impair). ADHD impairs the latter, not the former — which is why students can sustain attention on video games but not homework.

Assessment first: the **Executive Skills Questionnaire (ESQ)** identifies the 2–3 weakest skills. Coaching targets those specifically rather than trying to improve the full profile at once.

---

### Ch. 3 — The Coaching Session

Each coaching session follows the **REAP loop** (p.37, Ch.3):

1. **R**eview — what happened since last session; did the plan work
2. **E**valuate — why it worked or didn't; what needs to adjust
3. **A**nticipate — what's coming up; obstacles to plan around
4. **P**lan — build a specific, concrete plan for the next period

Sessions are short (15–20 min typically). Focus is behavioural and forward-looking, not insight-oriented. The coach does not prescribe — coach and student co-build the plan together.

Key principle: **correspondence training** (Paniagua, 1992). Having the student verbally commit to a plan ("say-do" correspondence) increases follow-through. The act of stating the plan out loud is itself part of the intervention.

---

### Ch. 4 — Goal Setting and Monitoring

Goals must be:
- Specific and concrete (not "do better in school")
- Student-chosen or co-created — goals imposed by adults do not stick
- Short time horizon — days to weeks, not months

**Goal attainment scaling** offers a structured way to track: a 5-level scale from −2 (much less than expected) to +2 (much more than expected), with 0 as the expected outcome. Allows nuanced tracking without binary pass/fail.

**Fading**: coaching frequency systematically reduced over time as the student demonstrates internalization. Typical 6-step schedule from daily check-ins → weekly → bi-weekly → monthly. Fading is planned from the start, not treated as graduation.

## Part II: School-Based Applications

### Ch. 5 — Setting Up a School-Based Coaching Program

10-step process (Table 5.1):

1. Solicit coaches (teachers, counsellors, other staff)
2. Train coaches in the model
3. Identify student candidates
4. Interview and invite students (voluntary participation)
5. Notify parents
6. Match student to coach
7. Conduct initial goal-setting session
8. Begin weekly check-ins
9. Group case consultation (coaches meet to problem-solve)
10. Evaluate and adjust

**Time requirement:** 45–60 minutes per student per week (includes prep, session, and documentation).

**Student identification criteria:** failing despite ability, labelled "lazy" or "unmotivated" by teachers, showing a drop-off in middle or high school. The drop-off matters — it signals the point where external scaffolding was removed and EF deficits became visible.

**Matching:** student preference outweighs administrative convenience. If the match is not working after 2–3 weeks, reassign without treating it as failure.

---

### Ch. 6 — Classwide Peer Coaching

Evidence base: 25+ studies (Greenwood et al., 2001); 12-year longitudinal study (Greenwood & Delquadri, 1995) showing sustained academic gains.

**7 essential steps for classwide peer coaching:**

1. Identify the target behavior
2. Set a goal
3. Outline the steps to reach the goal
4. Create a checklist or visual cue
5. Supervise (student-to-student)
6. Evaluate and revise
7. Fade

Two classroom activities described:
- **Math facts goal setting**: students set individual goals and check each other's progress
- **Independent work behavior goal setting**: students define what "on-task" looks like and rate each other

The peer-to-peer accountability loop replicates many features of formal coaching with lower staff burden.

### Ch. 7 — Peer Coaching for Social Skills (Plumer)

This chapter applies the model to social skills specifically — a domain where EF deficits are often most visible in real time.

Key Barkley (1997) distinction:

> "The problem [with ADHD] is not one of knowing what to do, but one of doing what they know when it would be most adaptive to do so."

This distinguishes ADHD from skill deficits. Students know the social rules. The EF impairment is in applying them at the right moment in context.

**Structure:**
- Triad: peer coach + focus student + adult supervisor
- Adult supervisor: ~30–45 minutes per week
- Teacher involvement: ~5–10 minutes per day

**Rating scale** (4-point daily):
- Honors (4)
- Satisfactory (3)
- Needs Improvement (2)
- Unsatisfactory (1)

**Reward tiers based on weekly average:**
- Average 3.0–3.9 → Reward tier 3
- Average 4.0–4.9 → Reward tier 2
- Average 5.0 → Reward tier 1 (best)

**Evidence:** Plumer & Stoner (2005) pilot study + Plumer (2007) ABAB single-case design. Not large RCTs, but controlled methodology.

---

### Ch. 8 — Parting Thoughts

Three themes:

**1. The self-determination goal.** The endpoint of coaching is not compliance — it is the student internalising the structure and running their own REAP loop independently. Parker & Boutelle (2009) qualitative study of college students who experienced coaching identified self-talk internalisation as a central outcome: "I started hearing my coach's voice in my head."

**2. What coaching actually provides.** Parker & Boutelle (2009) reported that students experienced: clarified goals, more positive self-awareness, attitude change, and reduced stress. Coaching was not primarily about technique — the relationship and personalisation were the active ingredient.

**3. Coaching ≠ tutoring.** Tutors transmit knowledge or skills. Coaches use questioning to elicit goals and strategies *from* the student. The distinction matters: students who wanted more didactic instruction reported dissatisfaction with coaching. It is not the right tool for knowledge gaps.

**Limitations acknowledged:** depression interfering with engagement; students forgetting sessions; results not fast enough for some; not yet ready to articulate goals.

## Evidence Assessment

**Tier 2 — Practitioner framework. Primary text read directly. No RCTs.**

The framework is extensively used in clinical and educational practice. The evidence base for specific components is thin:
- Classwide peer coaching: strongest evidence (25+ studies, 12-year longitudinal)
- Individual coaching model: qualitative support (Parker & Boutelle 2009) but no RCT
- Social skills peer coaching: controlled ABAB design — suggestive, not definitive

No published RCT compares coached Dawson/Guare to self-directed scaffolding or waitlist control.
