# Computerised Cognitive Training (CogMed and equivalents)

Documented here primarily to explain why it is not recommended as a standalone intervention for adults with EF deficits, despite being widely marketed.

---

## What It Is

Software programmes (CogMed being the most researched) that deliver repeated working memory exercises — adaptive difficulty tasks designed to train the ability to hold and manipulate information. Sessions run 30–45 minutes daily over 5 weeks.

---

## What the Evidence Shows

**Near-transfer is real.** People get better at the tasks they practise. Working memory test scores improve.

**Far-transfer is not.** Gains do not generalise to:
- Other EF domains (inhibition, flexibility)
- ADHD symptom reduction
- Real-world function (academic performance, daily organisation)

This is the central finding from two independent meta-analyses (Melby-Lervåg et al. 2016; Sala & Gobet 2019 — see [[scaffolding-vs-training]]). A 2025 CogMed-specific meta-analysis (11 RCTs, adults 18+) confirms: significant improvements in working memory immediately post-training; limited sustained benefit at 2–6 months; no generalisation to ADHD symptoms or other cognitive outcomes (PubMed 40581145).

**The gap between near- and far-transfer is not small.** Near-transfer effect size g≈0.46; far-transfer g≈0.12, approaching zero after correcting for publication bias.

---

## The Adult Implementation Problem

Beyond the generalisation issue, computerised training has a specific practical failure mode in adults with ADHD.

A feasibility study of CogMed in adults found only 38% completion (5 of 13 participants finished). Reported barriers:

- Sessions ran 55 minutes on average — far longer than the advertised 30–45 minutes
- Self-directed daily engagement required sustained EF — exactly what is impaired
- Weekly coaching calls were insufficient to maintain adherence
- Internet access reliability was a practical obstacle

The authors concluded that the programme's design assumes a level of self-regulation that adults with ADHD typically lack. Children have caregiver support; adults are expected to manage the programme themselves.

---

## Why It Remains Popular

- Commercially well-resourced and heavily marketed to schools and clinics
- Near-transfer results are real and measurable — look good in short-term outcomes
- Parents and practitioners want to feel something active is being done
- Costs less than CBT or coaching

---

## Verdict

Not recommended as a standalone intervention. The generalisation problem is fundamental, not a matter of programme refinement. Adults face an additional completion barrier that has not been solved.

The working memory resources are better spent on building external scaffolds that do the working memory work for you — see [[scaffolding-vs-training]].

**If computerised training is already in use:** Not harmful. Continue only if it is producing real-world benefits beyond test scores. Stop if it is not.
