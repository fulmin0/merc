# Exercise and Executive Function

Exercise has the most consistent and broad evidence for structural brain changes of any intervention in the EF literature. Other approaches compensate around the deficit; exercise changes the underlying hardware.

## What Changes Structurally

Aerobic exercise produces:
- More grey matter and better connectivity in the hippocampus (memory) and prefrontal cortex (executive control)
- Increased BDNF (a protein that supports brain cell survival and growth — think fertiliser for neurons)
- More efficient processing — the brain relies less on effort-heavy regions when doing EF tasks
- Strengthened inhibitory control networks

These aren't just correlations. The causal pathway is increasingly clear: exercise reduces brain inflammation, strengthens connections between neurons, and promotes the growth of new ones.

## What EF Domains Improve

Broad improvement — not task-specific. Inhibitory control, working memory, cognitive flexibility all show gains. This is the key distinction from cognitive training, which improves only trained tasks.

## Durability

Changes persist longitudinally when exercise is maintained. A 3-month aerobic program shows functional neuroplasticity effects on inhibitory control that persist post-training. The mechanism is structural — the changes are real, not just acute arousal effects.

If exercise stops, gains diminish over time. The substrate doesn't stay changed without maintenance.

## Comparison to Other Interventions

| Intervention | Structural change | Generalizes broadly | Durable |
|---|---|---|---|
| Exercise | Yes | Yes | Yes (with maintenance) |
| Medication | No — compensation | Partial | No — state-dependent |
| Cognitive training | Minimal | No — near-transfer only | No — fades post-training |
| Scaffolding | No | N/A (not building) | Yes (while in place) |

Exercise is the only row with three yeses.

## Optimal Protocol (Based on Meta-Analytic Evidence)

Best practice for EF gains, based on dose-response data from chronic intervention studies:

| Variable | Target |
|---|---|
| Type | Aerobic (running, cycling, swimming) — primary. Resistance as add-on. |
| Frequency | 3–5 sessions/week |
| Duration per session | 30–60 min |
| Intensity | Moderate-to-vigorous — breathing hard but still able to talk (60–80% max heart rate) |
| Minimum program length | 12 weeks for meaningful structural/functional change |

**12 weeks is the critical threshold.** Studies of 6–8 weeks show modest gains; 12+ weeks show substantially greater and more durable effects.

### Aerobic vs. Resistance
- **Aerobic** has the clearest evidence for EF specifically — inhibitory control, working memory, cognitive flexibility all improve
- **Resistance training** shows benefits especially for inhibitory control in older adults (12-month RCT, JAMA Internal Medicine)
- **Combined aerobic + resistance** outperforms either alone for attention, processing speed, and working memory
- Recommended: start aerobic; add resistance when the aerobic habit is established

### Acute vs. Chronic Effects
- A single session produces immediate EF improvements (reduces ADHD symptoms acutely)
- Chronic training produces structural changes that persist; acute effects are additional, not the primary mechanism
- Use acute sessions strategically: exercise before cognitively demanding tasks

## Limitation

Individual variation is high. Studies show population-level effects — any given person may respond more or less. Also: this is the hardest intervention to initiate for someone with severe EF deficits. Building an exercise habit requires exactly the EF capacity you're trying to build. The order problem is real — see [[scaffolding-vs-training]] for why scaffolding should come first.

## Open Questions

- Optimal type, frequency, and duration for EF specifically (aerobic vs. resistance, how much, how often)
- Dose-response relationship
- Whether gains are additive with medication or scaffolding
