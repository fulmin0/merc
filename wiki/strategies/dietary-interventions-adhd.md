# Dietary and Nutritional Interventions for ADHD

## Summary

Nutritional interventions are among the most popular non-pharmacological approaches for ADHD — and among the most poorly evidenced for adults. The evidence base is primarily in children. Most adult-specific data is absent; extrapolations from paediatric studies should be treated cautiously.

**Verdict in advance:** Omega-3 supplementation has the most support — modest, inconsistent, but real. Elimination diets have some paediatric evidence; adult evidence is negligible. Nothing in this category belongs in the primary stack; these are supplementary considerations once the main layers are in place.

---

## Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA)

### What and why
Individuals with ADHD show lower levels of omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs), particularly EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid) and DHA (docosahexaenoic acid), compared to non-ADHD controls. Omega-3s are structural components of neuronal membranes and modulate dopaminergic and serotonergic neurotransmission — pathways implicated in ADHD.

### Evidence

**Liu et al. (2023) — meta-analysis, 22 RCTs, n=1,789:**

- Overall: omega-3 PUFAs did **not** significantly improve core ADHD symptoms vs. placebo (SMD: −0.16; 95% CI: −0.34 to 0.01; p=0.07)
- Subgroup — studies ≥4 months duration: significant improvement (SMD: −0.35; 95% CI: −0.61 to −0.09; p=0.007)
- Neither high EPA dosage nor high EPA:DHA ratio consistently improved outcomes
- *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry*, PMID: 37656283

**Key caveat:** nearly all RCTs in this meta-analysis are in children and adolescents. Adult-specific data is minimal.

### What the evidence supports
- A modest, real effect appears with supplementation sustained ≥4 months
- Effect size (SMD ~0.35 at 4+ months) is small — comparable to a weak adjunct, not a primary treatment
- No harm documented at typical doses (1–3g EPA+DHA daily)

### What it does not support
- EPA-only or high-ratio formulations over standard fish oil
- Omega-3 as a substitute for medication or structured behavioural intervention
- Expecting meaningful EF changes in adults on current evidence

### Practical guidance
If considering: standard fish oil providing 1–2g EPA+DHA per day; sustained ≥4 months to assess effect; monitor alongside whatever other interventions are in place. Low risk, low cost, modest evidence.

## Elimination Diets

### What and why
The hypothesis: certain food additives (artificial colourings, preservatives) or foods (gluten, casein, sugar) exacerbate ADHD symptoms in sensitive individuals by triggering inflammatory responses or neurochemical disruption.

### Evidence
**The INCA trial (Pelsser et al., 2011) — most cited paediatric elimination diet RCT:**
- N=100 children; few-foods diet (restricted to rice, meat, vegetables, pears, water) vs. healthy control diet
- 64% of children on elimination diet showed ≥50% symptom reduction vs. 0% in control
- Restriction was extreme; highly impractical for real-world use
- Adult equivalent: no comparable RCT exists

**Artificial food colorings (AFC) meta-analysis (Nigg et al., 2012):**
- Small but significant effect of AFC removal on ADHD symptoms in children (d=0.18–0.42)
- Effect primarily in children; not studied in adults
- Likely most relevant for a minority with specific sensitivity — not a universal intervention

### What the evidence supports
- In children: a subset (possibly 30–60%) show meaningful symptom improvement with elimination diets
- AFC removal has the most controlled evidence and is the most practical starting point
- Sugar restriction: no controlled evidence for ADHD symptom reduction (the "sugar causes hyperactivity" belief is not supported by RCTs)

### What it does not support
- Routine elimination diet as a first-line intervention for adults
- Gluten or casein elimination without evidence of specific sensitivity (coeliac, intolerance)
- Expecting results comparable to medication

### Practical guidance
If interested: start with removing artificial food colourings (Red 40, Yellow 5/6, etc.) rather than broad elimination — more sustainable and has the most evidence. Track symptoms for 4–6 weeks. If no signal: no evidence for broader elimination unless specific food sensitivities are established.

## Iron and Zinc Deficiency

### Brief note
Studies in children show that iron and zinc deficiencies (below normal serum levels, not just low-normal) correlate with ADHD severity and respond to supplementation. Effect is specific to deficient individuals — supplementing adequate levels produces no benefit and may be harmful.

**Practical implication:** If fatigue, poor concentration, and restlessness are prominent — and have not responded to ADHD-specific interventions — basic blood panel (ferritin, serum zinc) is a reasonable step. Supplementation only if deficiency is confirmed. Not a general intervention.

## Caffeine

Not a nutritional intervention per se, but commonly self-used by adults with ADHD as a stimulant substitute.

- Adenosine antagonist; increases dopamine and norepinephrine release acutely
- One RCT (Stein et al., 2011): caffeine improved sustained attention in ADHD adults, effect significantly smaller than methylphenidate
- Late-day caffeine worsens sleep, which worsens EF — a net-negative if sleep is already compromised
- High tolerance develops rapidly

**Practical guidance:** Morning caffeine (before noon) is unlikely to cause net harm for most. Evening use risks compounding sleep deficits. Not a substitute for any other intervention.

## What Is Not Evidenced

| Claim | Evidence status |
|---|---|
| Sugar causes ADHD or hyperactivity | Not supported — multiple RCTs find no effect |
| Gluten/casein-free diet improves ADHD | No RCT evidence in non-coeliac adults |
| Specific "brain foods" improve EF | General healthy diet advice; no ADHD-specific RCT |
| Gut-brain axis supplements (probiotics) | Interesting mechanistic hypothesis; no adult ADHD RCTs |

---

## Stack Position

Dietary interventions are not in the main stack. They are low-effort, low-risk supplementary considerations once the primary layers (scaffolding, sleep, exercise, medication if applicable) are in place.

If investigating: omega-3 first, for ≥4 months. AFC removal next if motivated. Iron/zinc panel if clinically warranted.
