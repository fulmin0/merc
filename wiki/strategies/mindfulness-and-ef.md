# Mindfulness-Based Interventions and Executive Function

Mindfulness produces measurable improvements in attention, working memory, and emotional regulation. Not the strongest intervention in the stack — but one of the few with documented neural changes and a useful role in emotional regulation specifically.

## What the Evidence Shows

### General Cognitive Effects (111 randomised controlled trials, N=9,538)

A 2024 meta-analysis — a study pooling the results of 111 individual trials — found small-to-moderate effects on:

| Domain | Effect vs. waitlist | Effect vs. active control |
|--------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| Attention/executive attention | g=0.39–0.64 | g=0.19–0.39 |
| Working memory accuracy | g~0.42 | smaller |
| Inhibitory control accuracy | g~0.42 | smaller |

**Does not improve:** Processing speed, episodic memory, verbal fluency.

Effects are reliably smaller against active controls than waitlist — i.e., some of the benefit is from non-specific factors (attention, expectancy, group participation).

### ADHD-Specific Evidence

Two meta-analyses confirm mindfulness-based interventions reduce ADHD symptoms in adults and improve:
- Core ADHD symptoms (large self-report effect sizes: 1.26–3.14, per Cairncross & Miller meta-analysis cited below)
- Executive functioning ratings
- Emotional regulation
- Comorbid anxiety and depression

Caveats: ADHD RCTs are smaller and fewer than general-population trials. Gains are larger on self-report than on objective neuropsychological testing.

## What Changes in the Brain

Brain imaging studies document:
- Stronger connection between the prefrontal cortex and the amygdala (the brain's alarm system) — the circuit that governs emotional regulation
- Less activity in the default mode network during meditation — the network responsible for mind-wandering and rumination
- Better coordination in attention networks

Compared to exercise, mindfulness changes how the brain functions rather than its physical structure. The effects are real, but they work differently — rewiring patterns of activity rather than growing new tissue.

## Key Programs

**MBSR (Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction)** — 8-week structured program; body scan, sitting meditation, mindful movement. The most-studied format. Not ADHD-specific.

**MBCT (Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy)** — integrates CBT with mindfulness. Originally developed for depression relapse prevention; evidence for ADHD is growing.

**MAPS for ADHD** — Mindful Awareness Practices, UCLA. ADHD-targeted adaptation. Pilot RCT evidence only — not yet scaled.

## Where It Fits in the Stack

Primary use case: **emotional regulation and impulse control**. Secondary: sustained attention.

Not a substitute for scaffolding (doesn't reduce external EF demands). Works alongside scaffolding once basic systems are in place. Particularly valuable when emotional dysregulation is a primary presenting problem — Barkley's Self-Regulation of Emotion domain.

**Activation energy:** Moderate. Requires consistent daily practice; gains are not immediate. Start with 10–15 min/day structured program (MBSR or app-based). Gains appear at 4–8 weeks of regular practice.

## Limitations

- Gains require active maintenance — no evidence of durable improvements post-program without continued practice
- Effect sizes shrink against active controls; non-specific factors contribute
- Most research is in healthy or mildly clinical populations; ADHD-specific RCTs are underpowered
- Sample bias: predominantly white, educated, middle-class populations in the evidence base
