# Solanto's Meta-Cognitive Therapy (MCT)

The most rigorously tested non-medication intervention for adult EF deficits. Often called "CBT for Adult ADHD" — the formal name is Meta-Cognitive Therapy. Unlike standard CBT, the goal isn't to address emotional problems. It teaches compensatory skills and self-management systems.

Twelve sessions. Four modules. One real RCT.

## The Four Modules

### Module 1: Time Management (Sessions 2–6) — The Backbone

This is the core. Everything else builds on it.

- **S2 — Time awareness.** Visible clocks everywhere. Wristwatches. Time logs — track estimated vs. actual duration as data, not moral failing. Mantra: "Time passes whether or not I'm aware of it." Targets time blindness directly.
- **S3 — Planner.** One master planner. All tasks, specific times. Not a to-do list — a schedule. Rule: "If it's not in the planner, it doesn't exist."
- **S4 — Prioritization.** Urgent vs. important. Top 3 tasks daily. Protect high-priority time from low-priority requests.
- **S5 — Task initiation.** Task dismantling: break until the first step takes under five minutes. Rule: "If I can't start, the first step is too big." Contingent self-reward: pre-commit to a specific, immediate reward *before* the task, not after.
- **S6 — Cognitive restructuring** for blocking thoughts (one dedicated session).

### Module 2: Organization (Sessions 7–9)

- **S7** — Physical filing systems. Design principle: simple enough to maintain with impaired working memory. If a system requires remembering where things go, it will fail.
- **S8** — Email and information flow. Folder systems, "touch it once" principle, reducing inbox overload.
- **S9** — Routines. Daily habits that automate decisions that would otherwise require EF. Goal: behavior that doesn't depend on in-the-moment executive control.

### Module 3: Long-Term Planning (Sessions 10–11)

Addresses future-blindness — the inability to maintain direction toward distant goals despite normal intelligence.

Clients flowchart a significant goal into sub-components with interim deadlines, then schedule those sub-components into the master planner. The planning is behavioral, not aspirational.

### Module 4: Review (Session 12)

Recap all strategies. Identify persistent failures. Maintenance planning. No new content. Introduction of the 6-session booster (see Limitations).

## Cognitive Restructuring

Secondary mechanism — not the core. One dedicated session, then woven in when blocking beliefs surface.

Five belief categories targeted:
1. **Perfectionism** — "It has to be perfect or it's no good." Drives avoidance before the task begins.
2. **Catastrophizing** — "This is going to be overwhelming." Challenged by task dismantling + examining past completions.
3. **Demoralization** — "I'm fundamentally broken." Challenged with evidence from emerging successes.
4. **Procrastination justifications** — "I work better under pressure." Examined against actual outcomes.
5. **Performance anxiety** — Social evaluation fear driving avoidance. Standard decatastrophizing.

Method: guided questioning to examine the belief, written records to track the process. Identify the blocking thought → look at the evidence for and against → generate a more accurate alternative → notice how much you believe the original thought now.

Important: Solanto explicitly frames cognitive restructuring as addressing *blockades to skill deployment*, not the primary therapeutic mechanism.

## Procrastination: Two Separate Problems

**Initiation failure** — ADHD is associated with steep delay discounting: future consequences are neurologically devalued relative to immediate ones (PMC5049699, meta-analysis of case-control studies). The long-term cost of not starting feels abstract; the immediate relief of avoidance feels real and rewarding.
Interventions: task dismantling, pre-committed immediate reward, visualization of consequences, cognitive restructuring of avoidance beliefs.

**Completion failure** — driven by distractibility or perfectionism-induced stalling.
Interventions: sub-milestones with rewards, scheduled uninterrupted blocks, "good enough" cognitive restructuring.

## The RCT

Solanto et al. (2010), *American Journal of Psychiatry*, NIMH-sponsored. N=88 rigorously diagnosed adults. Randomized to 12-week MCT group vs. matched supportive therapy. Primary outcome assessed by blinded clinician.

| Metric | MCT | Support |
|---|---|---|
| Response rate (≥30% improvement) | **42%** | 12% |
| Odds ratio | **5.41** | — |
| Effect size | **~0.56 SD** (moderate) | — |
| Significant-other rating improvement | **-5.7 pts** | -0.9 pts |

Homework completion = single strongest predictor of outcome within MCT, per within-group analysis in Solanto et al. (2010) (PMC3633586). Dose-response: more homework done, more benefit.

Medication status did NOT moderate outcome. Works equally with or without stimulants — unexpected finding, theoretically important.

## Limitations

**No follow-up data.** Zero. Durability of gains is completely unknown. ADHD is chronic. Assuming 12 weeks of skill training produces self-sustaining habits is not validated — and the existence of the booster program implies it often doesn't.

**High-IQ sample.** IQ 119 (MCT group) and 114 (support group); pooled ~117. The protocol has not been tested in average-IQ or lower-functioning adults. Generalizability is limited.

**No dismantling studies.** Unknown which modules drive the effect. Could be time management alone. Could be the group format. Not known.

**Gender and subtype dropout.** Men dropped out at 48% vs. 27% for women. Combined-type dropped at 48% vs. 26% for Inattentive-type. The RCT may only be validating the protocol for women and inattentive-predominant adults.

**Active ingredient unclear.** The support group also improved (2.3 pts). Nonspecific factors — attention, normalization, hope — contribute. How much of the MCT effect is specific to skill-building is unknown.

**6-session booster program** exists (focuses on procrastination, prioritization, perfectionism) but lacks RCT validation.
