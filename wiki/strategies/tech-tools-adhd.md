# Tech Tools for Executive Function

A note on evidence first: there is very little research on digital tools for adults with ADHD specifically. Most digital intervention trials involve children. What evidence exists for adults is low quality and inconclusive. This page is organized around the underlying problems — which have strong scaffolding evidence — rather than specific apps, which change constantly and lack adult RCTs.

The tools here are practical implementations of the scaffolding principles in [[scaffolding-vs-training]].

---

## By Problem

### Time Blindness
*Can't feel time passing. Underestimates duration. Misses deadlines.*

The solution is to make time visible and concrete — not to try to feel it better.

| Tool type | What it does | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Visual countdown timer | Shows time as a shrinking shape — you can see it disappearing | Time Timer (physical or app), TimerClock |
| Time-blocking calendar | Allocates specific hours to specific tasks; makes the day concrete | Structured (app), Google Calendar with blocks |
| Pomodoro timer | Breaks work into fixed intervals (e.g. 25 min on, 5 min break) | Forest, Be Focused |

The physical Time Timer has the best practitioner endorsement for time blindness specifically — the visual feedback is more salient than a number countdown.

---

### Task Initiation
*Knowing what to do but being unable to start.*

The solution is reducing the activation energy — making the first step so small it's hard to avoid.

| Tool type | What it does | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Task manager with sub-tasks | Forces task decomposition into small visible steps | Todoist, Things, TickTick |
| Implementation intention prompts | Pre-schedules the first step at a specific time and place | Any calendar with detailed event notes |
| Commitment devices | Makes a public or semi-public commitment to start | Focusmate, BeeMinder |

Focusmate (body doubling — working on video with a stranger) has no RCT evidence for adults but is widely reported as effective. The mechanism — externalising motivation through a social presence — is consistent with established scaffolding principles.

---

### Capture and External Working Memory
*Forgetting things that aren't written down. Losing track of what needs doing.*

The solution is a frictionless capture system — one place, always accessible, very low effort to add to.

| Tool type | What it does | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Single inbox | One place for everything that needs to go somewhere | Todoist inbox, Notion, Apple Notes |
| Voice capture | Dictate a thought before it's gone | iOS Siri shortcuts, voice memos, Voicy |
| Quick-capture shortcut | One keypress to open a capture box | Alfred (Mac), Raycast |

The key principle is **one system, not five**. Multiple inboxes defeat the purpose — things fall between them.

---

### Distraction and Focus
*Pulled away from tasks by notifications, open tabs, or environment.*

| Tool type | What it does | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Website/app blocker | Removes access to distracting sites during work blocks | Freedom, Cold Turkey, 1Blocker |
| Notification silencer | Removes interruptions from the environment | Focus modes (iOS/macOS), Do Not Disturb |
| Music / sound | Consistent auditory environment to reduce context-switching | Brain.fm, white noise |

A 2024 study found that music with fast amplitude modulation improved sustained attention, with greater benefits for participants with higher ADHD symptom scores (Russo et al., *Communications Biology*, 2024 — [nature.com/articles/s42003-024-07026-3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s42003-024-07026-3)). Brain.fm uses this principle. Standard music playlists vary too much — consistency matters more than quality.

---

### Planning and Long-Term Goals
*Difficulty connecting daily actions to longer-term intentions.*

| Tool type | What it does | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Weekly review template | A fixed format for reviewing the past week and planning the next | Any notes app with a template |
| Horizon planning | Maps projects to calendar dates, not just a to-do list | Notion, Obsidian, paper |
| Progress tracking | Makes visible what has been done (important for ADHD motivation) | Streaks, Habitica, paper chains |

---

## What to Avoid

- **Too many tools.** Each new tool adds overhead and decision fatigue. One task manager, one calendar, one capture inbox.
- **Complex systems.** If maintaining the system requires EF, it will fail when EF is lowest — exactly when you most need it.
- **App-switching as procrastination.** Setting up productivity systems is often a way of avoiding the actual work.

---

## Evidence Note

Digital intervention research for adults with ADHD is sparse and low quality. A 2025 systematic review of 26 reviews found only 7 covered adults at all, and those with adult data reported inconclusive results. The scaffolding principles underlying these tools have strong evidence (see [[scaffolding-vs-training]]); the specific apps do not.

Evidence tier 4 here reflects this honestly — not that the tools don't work, but that app-specific adult trials don't exist yet.
