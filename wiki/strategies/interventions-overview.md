# Interventions Overview

**Core finding:** External scaffolding consistently beats internal training. Design around the deficit before trying to fix it. See [[scaffolding-vs-training]].

Six broad categories. Evidence levels noted. Most research is on children — adult evidence is thinner but exists.

## Categories

### 1. External Scaffolding
To-do lists, digital planners, task breaking, prioritization systems, reminders, environment design.

**Evidence:** Practical, widely used, low risk. Not flashy but probably the most immediately useful for adults.  
**Best for:** Time management, organization deficits.  
**Limitation:** Doesn't build underlying capacity — compensates around the deficit.

### 2. Organizational Skills Training (OST)
Structured programs that teach planning, tracking, and organization explicitly.

**Evidence:** Empirically supported. Improves organizational skills and inattention. Works in children as young as 6.  
**Best for:** Self-organization/problem-solving domain.  
**Limitation:** Formal programs may not translate well to adult self-directed use.

### 3. Physical Activity
Aerobic exercise, team sports, martial arts, yoga.

**Evidence:** Well-established. Improves attention, working memory, cognitive flexibility. Effect sizes are real.  
**Best for:** Broad EF improvement, especially inhibition and working memory.  
**Limitation:** Requires sustained habit. Benefits may diminish without continuity.

### 4. Mindfulness-Based Interventions
Meditation, deep breathing, metacognitive awareness practices.

**Evidence:** Solid for attention and emotional regulation. Neural changes documented.  
**Best for:** Self-restraint, emotional regulation domains.  
**Limitation:** Requires practice over time. Benefits aren't immediate.

### 5. CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy)
Targets dysfunctional thinking patterns, self-regulation, problem-solving.

**Evidence:** Well-established for adults with ADHD specifically. More generalizable than children's interventions.  
**Best for:** Self-motivation, emotional regulation, behavioral patterns.  
**Limitation:** Requires a therapist or structured program. Not a quick fix.

### 6. Computerized Training (e.g. CogMed)
Working memory training via software.

**Evidence:** Most researched digital intervention. Improves trained tasks but **limited generalization** — gains often don't transfer beyond the training context.  
**Best for:** Working memory specifically.  
**Limitation:** Weak generalization is a significant problem. Not recommended as a standalone.

---

## Cross-Cutting Principle
No single intervention is universal. Matching intervention to specific deficit matters. Repeated practice and progressive challenge required for any of these to stick.

## Medication (Compensation, Not Fix)

Stimulants (methylphenidate, amphetamine) increase dopamine/norepinephrine in the prefrontal cortex. This normalizes arousal in regions responsible for inhibition, attention, and timing. Neuroimaging confirms: medication restores signal in underactivated regions — it doesn't build new circuits.

Stop medication, baseline capacity reverts. State-dependent support. Not a standalone solution, but removes a real chemical bottleneck.

**Evidence:** Well-established mechanism. Doesn't replace behavioral scaffolding.

---

## Priority Order (for adults, based on evidence + practicality)
1. External scaffolding — start here. Lowest activation energy, immediate effect, no new habits required.
2. Physical activity — only intervention with structural brain changes. Layer in once basic scaffolding is in place.
3. CBT / MCT (Solanto) — best adult-specific evidence. Teaches you to build and maintain scaffolding systematically.
4. Mindfulness — especially for emotional regulation.
5. OST — if a structured program is accessible.
6. Computerized training — low priority given weak generalization.

Medication sits outside this order — it's a parallel layer, not a replacement for any of the above.
